    1. Article 8 of Council Regulation (EC) no 2201/2003 of 27 November 2003 concerning jurisdiction and the recognition and enforcement of judgments in matrimonial matters and the matters of parental responsibility, (The Regulation) provides as follows:-
      General jurisdiction
      The courts of a Member State shall have jurisdiction in matters of parental responsibility over a child who is habitually resident in that Member State at the time the court is seised.
    2. Article 16 of The Regulation provides as follows:-
      Seising of a Court
      1. A court shall be deemed to be seised:
      (a) at the time when the document instituting the proceedings or an equivalent document is lodged with the court, provided that the applicant has not subsequently failed to take the steps he was required to take to have service
      effected on the respondent;

or

(b) if the document has to be served before being lodged with the court, at the time when it is received by the authority responsible for service, provided that the applicant has not subsequently failed to take the steps he was required to take to have the document lodged with the court.
    3. It is necessary to determine the date on which the court was seised of proceedings, as general jurisdiction under Article 8 of The Regulation refers to habitual residence of a child at the time the court is seised.

    4. The subject matter of the within proceedings is F. F was born in July 2015 in Ireland. For the purposes of establishing jurisdiction, F’s habitual residence (if any) must be determined.

    5. F's mother is G. She is a citizen of a member state of the EU (hereinafter The Member State), other than Ireland.

    6. Two putative fathers of F have been named, D and B. G is not married to either D or B. G maintains that D is father. DNA testing has indicated that B is father. The DNA test result has not been formally proved and remains in dispute by G. D has been served with proceedings but has not participated in such proceedings. The Child and Family Agency were unaware of B until August 2016, and B was not served with what will hereafter be described as the 2015 proceedings.

    7. B has been notified of what will hereafter be described as the 2016 proceedings under the Child Care Act 1991 (The 1991 Act), and also of enquiries being carried out by the court pursuant to The Regulation. He has indicated by email to the solicitors acting on behalf of the Child and Family Agency, and in conversation with the guardian ad litem , that he has instructed solicitors in Ireland. To date, neither he nor any lawyer on his behalf has appeared in court or filed any application, even though on the court's direction he was specifically informed that the court would complete hearing The Regulation 2201/2003 matter on 14 February 2017.

    8. As far as is known to the court, both D and B reside in The Member State. As far as is known to the court, neither D nor B have had any contact with F.

    Date on which the court was seised of the proceedings

    28. On 14 February 2017, having allowed what I consider to be adequate opportunity for B or his lawyers to attend, I proceeded to decision on the issue regarding seizing of the court for the purposes of Article 16 of The Regulation.

    29. I had previously heard submissions in this regard, including submissions that the seizing of the court of the 2015 proceedings, in July 2015, was rendered continuous by virtue of the Child and Family Agency taking protective measures in respect of F (i.e. " voluntary care ") from birth to present, that therefore the court was seised in July 2015 and examination as to jurisdiction should be as to that date. Re M (a minor) 1994 FLR 577, and Derbyshire Co Co v F and C [2014] EWFC 26 at paragraph 36 were opened in this regard]. I had also heard submissions that the proceedings commenced in July 2015 had been completed, and that the court is now seised of the proceedings commenced in July 2016 and examination as to jurisdiction should be as to that date.

    30. I have considered all of the submissions made. I do not find that the continuance of protective measures (i.e. " voluntary care ") in the period from July 2015 to July 2016 can render the seisin of proceedings by the court continuous, in circumstances where the 2015 proceedings came to an end at the latest on 24 November 2015 by way of withdrawal of the appeal and the abandonment of the s. 18 of The 1991 Act application, and the 2016 proceedings did not commence until 29 July 2016 or thereabouts. The court was seized of the 2015 proceedings from 13 July 2015 or thereabouts, to at the very latest 24 November 2015. The court should have examined jurisdiction in 2015, but it did not. The court became seized again on 29 July 2016 of a new set of proceedings. The court took up examination of jurisdiction per The Regulation in the context of the 2016 proceedings. I hold that examination as to jurisdiction now should refer to the date on which the court was seised of the 2016 proceedings. The court was seised of the 2016 proceedings on a date between the 29th July 2016 and 9th August 2016 (hereinafter "the relevant date"). It is immaterial on the facts of this case as to which of that range of dates it was.

    Habitual residence

    31. The habitual residence of F (if any) on the relevant date must be determined.

    32. The parties have opened various cases regarding habitual residence.

    33. The test for habitual residence under The Regulation is generally accepted as being that set out in A (Case -523/07) ECJ.

    34. Para 33 of A (Case -523/07) ECJ states:-
      “....the physical presence alone of the child in a Member State, as a jurisdictional rule alternative to that laid down in Article 8 of the Regulation, is not sufficient to establish the habitual residence of the child.”
    35. Paras 37 to 42 of A (Case -523/07) ECJ state
      “37 The ‘habitual residence’ of a child, within the meaning of Article 8(1) of the Regulation, must be established on the basis of all the circumstances specific to each individual case.

38 In addition to the physical presence of the child in a Member State other factors must be chosen which are capable of showing that that presence is not in any way temporary or intermittent and that the residence of the child reflects some degree of integration in a social and family environment.
      39 In particular, the duration, regularity, conditions and reasons for the stay on the territory of a Member State and the family’s move to that State, the child’s nationality, the place and conditions of attendance at school, linguistic knowledge and the family and social relationships of the child in that State must be taken into consideration.
      40 As the Advocate General pointed out in point 44 of her Opinion, the parents’ intention to settle permanently with the child in another Member State, manifested by certain tangible steps such as the purchase or lease of a residence in the host Member State, may constitute an indicator of the transfer of the habitual residence. Another indicator may be constituted by lodging an application for social housing with the relevant services of that State.

41 By contrast, the fact that the children are staying in a Member State where, for a short period, they carry on a peripatetic life, is liable to constitute an indicator that they do not habitually reside in that State.
      42 In the light of the criteria laid down in paragraphs 38 to 41 of this judgment and according to an overall assessment, it is for the national court to establish the place of the children’s habitual residence.
      36. The parties have also referred to a number of other cases where the question of determination of habitual residence was dealt with including Mercredi v Chafe (Case C-497/10PPU) ECJ , A v A (Children: Habitual Residence) [2013] UKSC 60 , Derbyshire County Council V F and C , [2104] EWHC 26.

      37. In addition to the facts set out at paragraphs 5 - 9 above, in considering determination of habitual residence, the following additional matters are relevant.

      38. G has a child with D, namely R (born xxxx 2014). R was the subject of child protection proceedings in The Member State and was made the subject of a care order, apparently on 6 February 2015.

      39. G arrived in Ireland at the end of March 2015.

      48. After her arrival in Ireland, mother attended appropriately for maternity care.

F is an Irish national and holds an Irish passport since June 2016. In the absence of an affidavit of laws, it is not clear whether F has an automatic right to citizenship in The Member State - mother’s home country – such entitlement has not been conceded by mother.

      58. In the context of all of the above, the court has to consider its jurisdiction under The Regulation. The court, as set out, considers it proper to examine jurisdiction on the basis of being seized on or about 29 July 2016.

      59. A (Case -523/07) ECJ provides a non-exhaustive list or sample of factors that may be considered in establishing the child's habitual residence (essentially confirmed and followed in Mercredi v Chafe and the other cases referred to).
              a) Duration of stay:
              At the relevant date, F had been physically present in this country for F’s entire life, a period of approximately 13 months. The duration of F’s physical presence is significant, but is not determinative. As per para 38 of "A", In addition to the physical presence of the child in a Member State other factors must be chosen which are capable of showing that that presence is not in any way temporary or intermittent and that the residence of the child reflects some degree of integration in a social and family environment. [emphasis added].

              b) Regularity of stay
              There is no issue as to " regularity ", in the sense that both G and F are entitled to be in the jurisdiction.

              c) Conditions of stay
              For the purpose of this decision, I regard " conditions " as meaning physical conditions. The physical conditions of F's stay in the jurisdiction are that F has been cared for by foster carers since discharge from hospital post birth, and F’s " conditions " have therefore been good. G's " conditions" have been that she has stayed in hostel accommodation throughout.

              d) Reasons for the stay in the member state and the family's move to that state

              As set out at paragraph 43 above, I hold on the balance of probabilities that the primary reason for G travelling to Ireland was to avoid the attention of The Member State’s child protection authorities. I further hold that on the balance of probabilities the primary reason for G's stay in this state up to the relevant date was similarly to avoid the attention of The Member State’s child protection authorities. I further hold on the balance of probabilities that the primary reason for F to be present in the state from birth to the relevant date is because of G continuing to avoid the attention of The Member State’s child protection authorities in respect of F.

              e) The child's nationality
              As set out at paragraph 50, F is an Irish national. F holds an Irish passport since June 2016. In the absence of an affidavit of laws, it is not clear whether F has an automatic right citizenship of the Member State – such entitlement has not been conceded by mother.

              f) The place and conditions of attendance at school
              Not relevant given F's age

              g) Linguistic knowledge
              Not relevant given F's age, …and [REDACTED]
      60. The next matter in the non exhaustive list in A (Case -523/07) ECJ for consideration is "the family and social relationships of the child in the state".

      · At the relevant date, the extent of F's family and social relationships with F’s biological family was that F was having access with mother three times weekly, 2.5 hours on each occasion, under supervision. F was having no contact with F’s father, sibling, or any other relative except whatever contact may have occurred with F’s aunt in company with mother.

      · Having considered both A (Case -523/07) ECJ and Mercredi v Chafe , I am of the view that the reference to " family " in this context in those cases is to the birth families of the subject children of those cases. I refer to paragraph 54 of Mercredi , as follows:-

      54. As a general rule, the environment of a young child is essentially a family environment, determined by the reference person(s) with whom the child lives, by whom the child is in fact looked after and taken care of.

      In my view, the context indicates that the " reference person " in that case was mother, where the dispute was essentially between parents.

      · In A v A (Children: Habitual Residence) [2013] UKSC 60, at para 54 (vi) the social and family environment of an infant or young child is shared with those (whether parents or others) on whom he is dependent. Hence it is necessary to assess the integration of that person or persons in the social or family environment of the country concerned.
        This might be interpreted as including an infant's social and family environment with foster carers, (although I am by no means certain that such interpretation is correct). If that is a correct interpretation, then F has " some degree " of integration in a social and family environment with F’s foster carers. In my view however such family and social relationships as F has with his foster carers are fundamentally temporary in nature in that F’s placement with the foster carers is and always has been short term, and whatever attachments F may have with the foster carers will be ended when F’s current short term placement ends.

      61. Finally, in A (Case -523/07) ECJ , there is reference by way of example as to indicators of parental intention by reference to accommodation. As noted, G has apparently applied for social housing in Ireland. It does not appear that she surrendered any accommodation in the Member State, or indeed that she had any such accommodation to surrender. In any event, I do not doubt that it is mother's intention to remain in Ireland, and to seek to have F returned to her care in Ireland.

      62. In addition to the factors set out in the non-exhaustive list in A (Case -523/07) ECJ , I have also considered that F was in the care of the Child and Family Agency under s. 4 of The 1991 Act from 13th July 2015 to the relevant date. It follows that in that period, it appeared to the Child and Family Agency that F required care or protection that F was unlikely to receive unless taken into its care, and that mother accepted that this was the case. Nevertheless, during the period from 13th July 2015 to the relevant date, apart from carrying out a psychological assessment of G, there was no progress with long term planning for F. " Voluntary care " under s. 4 remained terminable at any time by G, and in that sense lacked any real stability.

      63. Considering matters as at the relevant date, in summary, physical presence alone does not determine habitual residence; the duration of F's presence in this jurisdiction, F’s nationality, and F’s social and family relationships with the foster cares (in so far as they are relevant) are supportive indicators of habitual residence having been established; the reason for F being present and staying in the state, and the reason for F’s mother being present and staying in the state, the minimal degree of family and social integration of F with mother and other relatives, the fact that F’s foster care placement is short term and that such attachments as F may have with the carers will end, the fact that F’s placement in the context of " voluntary care " lacks stability, the fact that the only long term plan in existence is placement with R in the Member State, are contra indicators of habitual residence having been established. On balance, considering all of these factors, I conclude that F’s presence in the state has not been established as " not temporary ", nor has there been the requisite degree of integration in a social or family environment, to establish habitual residence in Ireland.

      64. I must also consider Article 17 of The Regulation, which provides as follows:

    Examination as to jurisdiction

    I do not consider that the facts of this case, as set out, establish that F is habitually resident in another Member State.
